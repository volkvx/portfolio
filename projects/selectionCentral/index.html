Managing all product data at Amazon

Designing a new tool to manage all product across all categories, countries, regions, sellers.

Background

Amazon sells over a billion products all those products need to be managed. What appears as a simple product page to a shopper, is a complex array of many products, rules, algorithms, data feeds with data spread over many countries, merchants, and data sources. A tool to manage all these products was developed over a decade ago when only a few product categories were available on Amazon. This tool was called Global Item Management, or GIM. With Amazon growing, the complexity of the management system grew as well to include other countries, special content, third party sellers, and algorithms. What initially started as a tool to upload Excel spreadsheets with the product record updates, was still a tool to upload Excel sheets over a decade later but now it was used by thousands Amazon employees handling other stores (as Zappos, 6pm, MyHabit), managing digital and physical products and operating across many countries.

Goals for the redesign

▪ Improve accuracy of products sold on Amazon by making it easier to manage products that Amazon sells.

▪ Improve efficiency and reduce errors of modifying existing products and entering new ones.

▪ Improve learning experience for users of the product management tool and reduce menial and repetitive tasks.

▪ Improve efficiency and reduce errors of modifying existing products and entering new ones.


Previous state of the Global Item Management (GIM) system

Job satisfaction for users using the previous GIM tool was one of the lowest at Amazon. The average employee turnover was a little over a year. The complexity and inefficiency of the GIM tool was partly to blame. A simple correction to the product data required generating an Excel spreadsheet, uploading it into a convoluted tool and offered little feedback throughout the process. Every manual step was error prone. The resulted high error rate and the competitive environment made most quit within a year.

Redesign project – Selection Central

The project to redesign the Global Item Management tool took less than a year of one designer (me) to complete. The new redesigned tool was called Selection Central and it was owned by one of the 50 teams in the catalog management space. None of these other teams had a UX designer, so it was the first attempt to bring user-centric approach to solve a complex problem that was not directly facing external customers. For most of the time designing the Selection Central, there wasn’t a Project Manager available, most of the cross-team collaboration, prioritization, feature definition relied on the UX designer and the development team. As part of the design process, about 2000 screen mocks were created, 30 clickable prototypes, 10 user studies, 2 surveys reaching out to thousands of users. The new Selection Central design significantly simplified the product editing experience over previous GIM system. Many common tasks saw an over 10x improvement in completion speed. No longer users were required to upload everything through Excel, but now could make edits directly in the tool. The number of tabs reduced from over 20 to 3. New visualizations allowed for more efficient and accurate editing. Despite all the simplification, the previous templates and tasks were supported. Previous tool required over 10 hours of intensive training and the user studies of the new designs showed that it was intuitive for most users to start using without any prior training. All originally set goals for the improved design were achieved.

Retail experience

A detail page for a single product on Amazon pulls data from different sources. The specific title can come from one vendor, description from another, pictures for the same product may come from a third. Such data can come in a form of constant feeds that may need manual intervention, or overrides. Most products are related to each other in some way, and those relationships need management. The purpose of the designed tool called Selection Central was to manage all underlying product data.

Reducing existing complexity

Focus on the user

To significantly improve the original Global Item Management (GIM) product, it was not enough to better rearrange menus. A new approach was needed by focusing on the user. Interviews were conducted to understand not only the exact SOPs, but also the reason for any corrections, the signals that make users enter the tool in the first place. The interviews focused on understanding the exact goals of the users, the work schedule, variances, the stakeholders. Most information was gathered by contextual inquiries and breadth was verified over surveys.

Earlier iterations

New editable grid

An editable grid was the key component of the new design. It allowed editing data directly without uploading Excel sheets. My design focus was not so much at automating the exiting processes, but rather how to preemptively alert the user that something is off AND execute or propose corrections automatically. Due to the internal data complexity it was tricky to design a format that would be both intuitive and scalable. The creative solution came from a way of breaking a result set into multiple tables.

The logic behind the grid

The grid had to support ALL products that are sold on Amazon. That includes handling over 8000 attributes, digital or physical products, from socks to electronics. Designed logic was thoroughly documented that went beyond "happy path". For the designs to be solid, it needed to intuitively handle cases of mismatched ASINs, wrong categories, missing or restricted.

Visualizing Parent-Child relationships

Most relationships among the products have a concept of Parent-Child. It was challenging to visualize hierarchical relationships in a flat table that supports reordering items. Initial design concepts failed in properly preserving the logic.

Security details

Focusing solely on a "happy path" during the design often misses important details of the user experience when something goes wrong. I pushed all designs into verifying scalability and access. Access control is an often overlooked area and was previously handled by an error popup. The new designs identified all possible cases where an access can be restricted and implemented UX with those restrictions in mind.

Redesigning relationships

Most relationships among the products have a concept of Parent-Child and the distinction is critical. Previous design didn't offer an intuitive way of navigating the relationship hierarchies.

New relationship UX concept

Redesigning vendor contributions

Different vendors can provide information about the same product. Even though the product is the same, the details, as Title and Description could be different. The system makes a choice what record to display from which vendor. Sometimes, however, an override to the system's choice needs to be made.

New vendor contributions

Working with large sets and data export

It is possible to export large data sets and manipulate records offline and upload the changes later. When the results are too large, export data becomes the only option. Designing a system that intuitively handles both online editing and export was a challenge.

Long running actions

Previously, a screen to enter a batch task was different from the screen where a list of running or completed tasks was shown. A better approach is to combine those so that entering a new task produces an instant feedback showing that it now becomes running. The actions and the history list was fully redesigned.
